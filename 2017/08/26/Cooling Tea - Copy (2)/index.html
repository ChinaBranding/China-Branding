Cooling Tea

Wow, it’s hot!

‘How can you discuss tea at such a time?’ you may ask.

‘I want my magazine to transport me somewhere far away… ‘At least we could talk
about some refreshing drinks.’

For many westerners, refreshing might mean a cold drink - possibly something
with a fruity bite. And for some Americans, that might actually include iced
tea.

Ambient heat saps energy. So it makes a kind of sense to compensate for that
energy slump using sugar (dissolved energy) and citrus extracts (often
considered reviving). Caffeine, theoretically, should also make us feel less
like wilting.

But what if we could do more than ‘compensate for’ the ravages of heat? What if
we could ‘expel’ heat? That’s the aim of many Chinese medicine tips offered in
summer.

There’s a tradition of drinking green tea in these months. Part of it stems from
the need to consume a perishable product at its most fresh. And green tea is
also considered to be an inherently ‘heat-reducing’ (下火的) ingredient - a
characteristic also ascribed to certain bitter (mostly green) vegetables. It’s
because of this that you’ll often hear people in China say that in hot weather
you need to brew your green tea even hotter. Certainly, the bitter (astringent)
aspects of a green tea come to the fore when you really scald those leaves.

The immediate effect of drinking something hot while being hot is strange; it’s
only later – once sweat is accelerated – that one feels anything like relief.
It’s also at these higher brewing temperatures that more caffeine is released.
That – I suggest - is part of the relieving effect.

But there are also some other compounds at work – particularly theanine – which
is associated not with stimulation but with alleviation of stress sensations.
(Probably quite useful these days). Highly volatile, theanine is known to be
absorbed by the body less efficiently when drunk cold than hot. So I am probably
missing out on a lot of theanine, for my preference these days is to drink green
tea from unheated water, topping it up and glugging until I have regained
everything sweated out at night (that’s whole pints before peeing). Nevermind
the reticence about hot liquid in hot weather; in the morning I’m just too
impatient to wait for it to heat.

Additionally, we’ve recently been experimenting with frozen tea lollies /
popsicles.

So how well does it work? Well, the lack of sweetener is probably more of a
polarizing issue frozen than it is with the cold bottled drinks (东方树叶) we
discussed earlier this year. The other (related) issue is the distribution of
flavour; the freezing process reminds us that tea is a suspension of solid
compounds in water, rather than a blanket modification of H2O. The tea flavor
gathers mostly in the tip of the ice pop – as well as in some craggy seams. So
far, darker leaves – like the recently-mentioned 大红袍 Dà Hóng Páo - seem to
make for better ice lollies than do green teas.

I hope that cooled you down a little. If not, try waving your magazine as a fan.
